## Three Answers to Three Likely Questions

### Q: "What makes it different from LangGraph or AutoGen?"

> Those are orchestration frameworks — they manage *flow*. arifOS manages
> *authority*. Think of it as the difference between a traffic light (orchestration)
> and traffic law (constitutional). LangGraph can sequence steps. arifOS can
> refuse to take a step if it violates a constraint — and that refusal is
> enforced by the kernel, not just logged.

### Q: "What problem were you trying to solve?"

> I watched a great institution slowly forget what it was built for.
> I'm a geologist, not a coder — I found my way to AI through the problem
> of governance. The question wasn't "how do we make AI do more" but
> "how do we make AI do the right thing when the incentives say do the
> wrong thing." That's what constitutional floors are for.

### Q: "Why industrial asset operations?"

> Because that's where the stakes are real. A drilling decision, a
> maintenance schedule, a pressure threshold — these aren't chatbot
> conversations. The governance problem in industrial AI is different
> from consumer AI: you can't just say "I'm not sure, let me ask a human."
> You need the system to know when it *should* say that. That's what
> AssetOpsBench tests, and that's what I want to learn from.

## If He Asks About Technical Details

The constitutional kernel exposes 7 tools:
- **init** — start a governed session
- **observe** — gather evidence
- **think** — reason with uncertainty labeled
- **route** — send intent to the right specialist agent
- **critique** — stress-test the plan
- **judge** — render a constitutional verdict
- **act** — execute (only after judge says yes)

Every action is an envelope with: who asked, what they wanted, what the
system decided, what evidence it used. The audit trail is append-only.
